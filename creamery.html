Chattaroy Cheese Co. - Creamery Operations, Pasteurization & Regulated Water

Chattaroy Cheese Co. was our family-owned goat dairy and creamery that my mother opened for business during my high school senior year. Shortly after kids popped, production started there, and the work was operational from day one: sanitation, workflow, equipment use, and system reliability under daily demand.

This was not build-out or planning work. It was learning how a regulated food-production system behaves once it is running.

The farm's well system was regulated as a public water system associated with commercial food production. Water quality, reliability, and disinfection were not background utilities; they were part of the compliance envelope the creamery operated inside. Water touched everything: sanitation, equipment cleaning, pasteurization, product safety, and regulatory exposure.

Production centered on batch pasteurization and cheese-making workflows, using a glycol-based pasteurization system. At the time, only my mother and I were authorized operators for that system. I was the youngest Licensed Dairy Pasteurizer Technician in Washington State, authorized by the Department of Health.

That authorization carried real responsibility. Pasteurization required correct temperature control, timing, verification, and repeatable sanitation between batches. The system had to be operated correctly, cleaned correctly, and documented consistently to keep production compliant and moving.

Production also began outside the building. The creamery was supported by a herd of roughly sixty goats, including milking does that were the literal source of the product. Managing livestock health, barn conditions, and milking created a constant boundary between barnyard conditions and food-grade space. Moving animals to milk - and bringing the barnyard back with them - made that clean/dirty boundary continuous and unavoidable.

Daily work involved sanitation discipline, clean-to-dirty transitions, stainless process equipment handling, washdown procedures, batch timing, material handling, and throughput pressure. Hose routing, drain behavior, access paths, and water availability either supported safe production or became immediate failure points.

I grew up in livestock environments through a lifelong 4-H background, and that context mattered. Animals introduce variability, waste, and contamination risk. Systems that succeed do not ignore that; they manage it through separation, process control, and consistent sanitation practice.

That experience established an early operational bias that carries through later work: systems that appear correct at install often fail under daily use. When animals, regulated water, pasteurization, sanitation, and production intersect, operational reality shows up fast.
