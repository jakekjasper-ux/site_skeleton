Regulated Food Production & Public Health Operations

(Licensed Dairy Processing)

Before much of my technical fieldwork, I worked inside a regulated food production environment where safety, compliance, and repeatability were non-negotiable.

I was licensed by the Washington State Department of Health as a dairy pasteurizer technician, responsible for operating and maintaining FDA Grade A dairy processing equipment. That role carried direct responsibility for sanitation protocols, temperature control, equipment integrity, and adherence to documented procedures designed to protect public health.

The work required understanding not just how systems function mechanically, but how small deviations compound into serious risk. Equipment wear, cleaning shortcuts, calibration drift, or procedural ambiguity could result in contamination, failed inspections, product loss, or illness. There was no tolerance for improvisation without analysis.

Beyond equipment operation, I served as a point of accountability between production staff, inspectors, and ownership. That meant translating regulatory requirements into practical workflows, ensuring procedures were followed under real production pressures, and maintaining documentation that reflected actual practice rather than idealized intent.

Operating in this environment reinforced several principles that have carried through all of my later work:

  • Systems must be designed for repeatability, not heroics
  • Procedures only work if people can realistically follow them
  • Compliance failures are often operational failures, not technical ones

This experience shaped how I think about safety-critical systems more broadly. Whether the context is potable water, electrical controls, or structural remediation, the same dynamics apply: clarity, discipline, and alignment between documentation and reality matter more than sophistication alone.
