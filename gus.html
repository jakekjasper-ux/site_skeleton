Structural and Bathroom Reconstruction

Structural correction, flush shower pan, and waterproof rebuild in a compromised attic bathroom.

Recurring leaks, cracked tile, and uneven floors traced back to severed joists and a dormer that leaned on a window opening instead of a continuous load path. The client required a zero-lip, flush shower pan—only feasible if stiffness, slope, and waterproofing were engineered together.

Failure and Constraints

Selective demo revealed three cut joists and a dormer bearing on the window frame, guaranteeing movement and waterproofing failure. Demo views document the cavity opened up to confirm load discontinuity and plan pan centering. Additional constraints: a zero-lip, curbless pan centered in the footprint, a low-profile bath fan in a tight ceiling, and a pocket door needing stable framing and plane control so hardware stays aligned.

Structural Correction

Restored the load path before touching finishes: sistered cut joists, rebuilt the dormer tie-in, and corrected subfloor plane and stiffness so the curbless pan would stay flat and drain correctly over time.

Waterproofing and Slope Control

Curbless pan was set on the corrected subfloor, then slopes and penetrations were locked in before membrane. Waterproofing followed structure—never used to mask movement. Slope geometry was verified before closing with tile.

Tile and Finish Verification

Tile and finishes were installed only after structure, slope, and waterproofing were verified. Each phase was inspected before close-in to prevent movement from telegraphing into the finish layer.

Result

Finished attic bath sits on a stable, level substrate with a flush shower entry, repaired dormer tie-in, and waterproofing sequenced to the corrected structure.

Key takeaways

  • Restored cut joists and dormer ties before any finish work to re-establish the load path.
  • Curbless pan set on a stiff, corrected subfloor; slopes and penetrations verified prior to membrane.
  • Waterproofing sequenced after structure and slope were stable, not used to hide movement.
  • Each phase inspected before close-in, preventing movement from printing into tile and fixtures.
  • Finished bath demonstrates structural fixes, not just surface repair, with serviceable niches and penetrations.
